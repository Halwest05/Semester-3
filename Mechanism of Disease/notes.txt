Cell injury:
	* Is a function of 
		1- Injury => type, duration, severity
		2- Cell => type, status, adaptability

	Causes:
		1- Physical
			* Electricution, extreme cold or heat

		2- Immune
			* Autoimmune diseases

		3- Infection
			* Viruses, bacteria, fungi, protozoa

		4- Nutrientional
			* Excess or defficient

		5- Chemical
			* Drug overdose, alcohol

		6- Hypoxia
			1- Ischemic: Local or Systemic
			2- Hypoxemia

		6- Aging
		7- Genetic

	Free Radicals:
		* Highly reactive, unstable chemicals
		
		Generation occurs by:
			1- Exogenous toxins
			2- Endogenous metabolic reactions
			3- Exposure to radiation

		Is removed by:
			1- self-decaying
			2- Anti-Oxidants (Vitamin ACE, Glutathione)
			3- Storage proteins (Ferritin, Transferrin)
			4- Enzymes

		Injure cells by:
			1- Membrane lipid peroxidation
			2- Interaction with proteins
			3- DNA Damage

	Cell injury mechanism can classified into:
		1- ischemic, hypoxic cell injury
		2- free radical cell injury
		3- chemical induced cell injury
		4- other mechanisms

	* Normal cell funcs depend on integrity of:
		1- Oxidative phosphorylation of adp to atp (source of energy in mitochondria)
		2- Sodium ionic pump at plasma membrane
		3- protein synthesis by ribosome
		4- Nuclear genetic apparatus and dna coding.

	Mild ischemia or hypoxia:
		1- Low level of atp => Defect sodium ionic pump => disturbances of permeability of cell membrane => influx of sodium => influx of water => cellular swelling (Hydropic degeneration) => Fall of microfilaments
		2- Clumping of chromatin (due to low ph (bcz increase in co2 makes solution acidic))

		* This is reversible up to an extent
		* Called Reversible cell injury, hydropic swelling, sublethal cell injury
		* Ex/ Angina pectoris, Fatty change of liver

	Severe ischemia or hypoxia:
		* Aerobic respiration shifts to anaerobic respiration

		1- (H20 + Nitrogen products) Increase (result in excess swelling in addition to defect of sodium ionic pump)
		2- (CO2 + Ketone body + Lactic acid) Increase (Lead to excess acidosis)
		3- (Pyknosis, Karyorrhexis, Karyolysis) => Nuclei loss
		4- Calcium influx into cytoplasm => Excess Calcification of mitochondria (loss of respiratory function)
							   => Self-digestion of lysosomes

		5- Cytoskeletal damage

		* This is irreversible cell injury (necrosis)
		* AKA irreversible cell injury, lethal cell injury, cell death
		* EX/ Myocardial infarction, lung infarction, brain infarction, renal infarction

General Inflammation:	(Prerequisite to acute inflammation and chronic inflammation)
	* 2 types of inflammation:
		1- Acute Inflammation
		2- Chronic Inflammation

	* Inflammation involves 2 basic processes:
		1- inflammatory response
		2- Healing process

	* Typical inflammation reaction occurs through a series of sequential steps:
		1- The offending agent, located in extravascular tissues, is recognized by host cells.
		2- Leukocytes, plasma proteins will flow towards offended site.
		3- Leukocytes, plasma proteins are activated and work together to eliminate the offender.
		4- The reaction is controlled and terminated.
		5- The damaged tissue is repaired.

	Features of Acute and Chronic Inflammation:
						Acute				Chronic
		Onset				Fast, in mins or hours	slow, in days

		Cellular infiltrate	Mainly neutrophils	Monocytes, macrophages, lymphocytes

		Tissue injury		Mild				Severe and progressive

		Signs				Prominent			Less

	Five Cardinal signs of inflammation:
		1- Redness
		2- Swelling
		3- Heat
		4- Pain
		5- Loss of function

	Killing mechanisms:
		1- ROS
		2- Reactive nitrogen species
		3- Lysosomal enzymes

Acute Inflammation:
	Causes:
		1- Microbial Infections
		2- Tissue necrosis
		3- Hypersensitivity
		4- Foreign bodies
	Hallmark:
		1- Exudation of fluid
		2- Infiltration of cells
	
	* Transudate has low protein content, Exudate has high protein content.

	Chemical mediators of acute inflammation:
		1- Proteases:
			1- Kinins
			2- Complement System C3a, C5a
			3- Coagulation / Fibrinolytic system
		2- Prostaglandins
		3- Cytokines

	Cytokines in Inflammation (All are in macrophages):
		1- TNF
		2- IL-1
		3- IL-6
		4- Chemokines

	Known few mediators in each major steps of inflammation:
		1- Vascular dilation: Histamine, Prostaglandins
		2- Vascular permeability: Histamine, Bradykinin
		3- Emigration of neutrophils: C5a, Leukotrine B4

	Migration of neutrophils:
		1- Margination
		2- Rolling
		3- Adhesion
		4- Emigration

	Key steps of acute inflammation:
		1- Transiet-Vasoconstriction
		2- Vasodilation
		3- Increasing permeability
		4- Stasis
		5- Margination
		6- Rolling
		7- Adhesion
		8- Emigration
		9- Chemotaxis
		10- Phagocytosis
		11- Damage to host tissue, Local and/or systemic
		12- Resolution (Repair)

	Disorders:
		1- Hereditary angio-edema
		2- Alpha-1 antitripsin defficiency
		3- Inherited complement deficiencies
		4- Defects in neutrophils function
		5- Defects in neutrophils number

Chronic Inflammation:
	How does it occur:
		1- Take over acute inflammation
		2- Start de novo
		3- Rise alongside acute inflammation

	Causes:
		1- Pesistent Infections
		2- Hypersensitivity
		3- Prolonged exposure to toxic agents
	
	Features:
		1- Infiltration with mononuclear cells (Macrophages, lymphocytes, plasma cells)
		2- Tissue distruction
		3- Attempts at healing by connective tissue replacement

	Cells & Mediators in chronic inflammation:
		1- Macrophages
		2- Lymphocytes
		3- Mast cells
		4- Eosinophils
		5- Neutrophils

	Functions of macrophages:
		1- Phagocytosis
		2- Presentation of antigens
		3- Synthesis of cytokines
		4- Control other cells via cytokine release

	Pathways of macrophage activation:
		1- Classic pathway
		2- Alternate pathway

	* Some activated macrophages may fuse, forming multinucleated giant cells.

	Systemic effect of chronic inflammation:
		1- Fever
		2- Leukocytosis
		3- Acute phase proteins

	* Granulomatous inflammation: a form of chronic inflammation with granuloma.

	Diseases with granulomatos inflammation:
		1- Tuberculosis
		2- Siphilis	
		3- Crohn Disease
		4- Leprosy

Hemostasis and Thrombosis:
	* Hemostasis: a series of regulatory process to forming blood clot so to stop bleeding.

	Steps of hemostasis:
		1- Arteriolar vasoconstriction
			* to reduce blood flow to injured site, mediated by endothelin factor.

		2- Primary hemostasis: Formation of platelet plug with these steps:
			1- Platelet adhesion
			2- Platelet shape change (rounded disc => Spiky flat plate)
			3- Platelet release granule (ADP, TXA2, Serotonin)
			4- Recruitment of more platelets
			5- Aggregation (Hemostatic plug)
			
		3- Secondary hemostasis: Deposition of fibrin.
			1- Tissue factor activates Factor VII, which involves in thrombin generation.
			2- Thrombin cleaves fibrinogen into fibrin, which consolidates the platelets plug.
			3- Phospholipid complex makes a negative charge, which activates Factor XII.

		4- Clot stablization and resorption.
			* t-PA blocks coagulation cascade and stimulate tissue repair
